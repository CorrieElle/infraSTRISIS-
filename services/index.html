How the Audit Works

1

Feasibility Gate

Before scheduling an audit, we confirm three things:

  • The business problem is real enough to matter.
  • The evidence needed for the audit is available.
  • Leadership has enough capacity or authority to act on what the audit reveals.

If the engagement doesn't pass those gates, we pause, narrow the scope, or recommend a better starting point.

2

Intake and Evidence Review

We gather context around the operating issue, the people involved, the workflows affected, and the decisions leadership needs to make.

3

Diagnostic Analysis

We examine the evidence for structural patterns, friction points, visibility gaps, and operational risk signals.

4

A Clear Written Picture

Leadership receives a clear written summary of what's slowing execution, what risks are most important, and what decisions should come next.

5

Decision Support

The audit closes with a practical action path so leadership can decide what to address first, what to monitor, and what to defer.

Before Your Audit

The quality of the diagnostic depends on the quality of what you bring to it. Before your audit, you'll share a few things so the conversation starts from your actual situation, not from scratch.

What's Not Working

What you're observing, what triggered the concern, and what success looks like from where you sit.

How You're Structured

A picture of who reports to whom, so we understand how work actually moves.

What You've Already Tried

What's been attempted and what happened, so we don't recommend what's already been ruled out.

We review everything in full before your session, so the diagnostic starts from where you already are.
